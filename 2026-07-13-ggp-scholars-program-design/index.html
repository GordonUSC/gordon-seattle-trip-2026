The thinking

Four efforts, one tension, one throughline

Consolidation is settled: the remnants of CYSTEM, GGP Scholars, the new Carter Lipscomb memorial strand, and the Games for Change scholars fold into one program. The design question is harder than the org chart. You asked for four things at once, and two of them pull against each other:

AI empowerment
Be the leaders. Every scholar learns to direct AI, not fear it.
Preserve the values
The mentorship warmth and belonging the grants were built on.
Fund from both sides
Attractive to personal foundations and to corporations alike.
Perceptibly elite
The apex of the Directory, unmistakably top of the field.

Elite and belonging look like opposites. Prestige usually comes from who you turn away; belonging comes from who you welcome. If we chase elite the ordinary way, we betray the grant values. So the whole design rests on refusing that trade, and on one idea that turns the tension into an engine.

The core idea

Elite is a result we produce, not a door we guard

GGP is prestigious because a scholar comes out elite, with the deepest support, the strongest mentorship, and the best outcomes in the field. Not because only a chosen few get in.

Selectivity by depth, never by scarcity. We go far with each scholar, we do not brag about who we declined. That is how a program reads as top of the Directory while staying true to belonging.

And the differentiator that makes GGP leaders in AI empowerment is the same move you make every day with me. Every scholar learns to direct AI with a human always in the loop, so they are amplified, not replaced. They leave as the most AI-fluent underestimated talent in games. That is the promise no other program in the Directory is making, and it is the one a corporation and a family foundation will both pay to be part of.

How the tension resolves into funding

Named funds give a personal foundation intimacy, a scholar to know, a name to honor, a story that is theirs. Cohort and track sponsorships give a corporation scale, brand at the apex of the field, and mentoring seats for its people. The Directory is the frame that makes the whole thing perceptibly elite: GGP is the capstone the entire pipeline points toward.

Three versions, stack ranked

Same values and AI throughline, three shapes

Each preserves the grant values and centers AI empowerment. They differ in how elite, how costly, and how they attract money. Working names; the program name itself is still your call.

Rank 1 · Recommendedbalances all four goals, durable after Houser

The Guild

One elite brand. Four ways in. A lifelong arc from Scholar to Fellow to Mentor.

Structure

One program, one application, routed into the four tracks by fit. A three-stage arc: Scholar (the year), Fellow (a second-year build + paid opportunity), Mentor (alumni give back). The guild metaphor is craft mastery and belonging at once.

AI empowerment

Every scholar ships one AI-empowered capstone and learns human-in-the-loop direction. An "AI in the Studio" module, co-built with Take-Two, sits at the center, not the edge.

Who funds it

Personal foundations: named scholarships (Carter Lipscomb is the first) with meet-your-scholar intimacy. Corporations: named cohorts, track sponsorships, mentoring seats.

Why it reads elite

Depth of support and published outcomes, the GDC capstone, and a Fellow tier that signals a career, not a certificate. Apex of the Directory without turning anyone away.

Cost + effort

Moderate. Reuses CYSTEM's mentorship spine and the existing GDC infrastructure. Runnable by the current team.

Risk

The four tracks must feel like one program, not four logos under a banner. Managed by the shared application, mentor pool, and capstone.

Verdict: the best balance of every goal you set, and the only one that is clearly affordable and durable the year an anchor funder leaves. Lead with this.
Rank 2most prestigious, highest cost and risk

The Academy

A named institute: resident AI lab, guest faculty from the studios, a demo day the industry attends.

Structure

A small, high-touch institute with named faculty, studio residencies, and a public capstone showcase. Fewer scholars, deeper immersion, a curriculum with a masthead.

AI empowerment

A resident AI lab is the flagship: scholars build alongside a named "AI studio," the most visible AI-leadership statement of the three.

Who funds it

Corporations: irresistible naming (the AI Lab, the Academy itself). Personal foundations: named chairs and faculty seats, prestige giving.

Why it reads elite

Most perceptibly elite of the three. Faculty, residencies, and a demo day carry obvious prestige.

Cost + effort

High. Faculty, space, an AI lab, and a showcase are real budget and staff, hard to stand up the year Houser exits.

Risk

Formality can chill the warmth. "Academy" can read as gatekept, the exact failure the core idea warns against. Slowest to launch.

Verdict: the aspirational north star. Grow The Guild into this once a corporate naming partner underwrites it. Right destination, wrong starting line for 2026-27.
Rank 3widest reach, least elite

The Constellation

Network first: many scholars, a shared AI toolkit, a light touch, belonging at scale.

Structure

A large, open network with a common AI toolkit and community, lighter mentorship, more people served for less per person.

AI empowerment

AI literacy for everyone, broad reach, shallower depth. Empowerment as access rather than mastery.

Who funds it

Corporations: reach and headcount stories. Personal foundations: harder, less intimacy, fewer named scholars to know.

Why it reads elite

It does not, by design. Its virtue is inclusion and scale, not prestige.

Cost + effort

Lowest per scholar. Cheapest to run.

Risk

Fails the "perceptibly elite" goal and weakens named-gift appeal. Depth of outcome, the thing funders renew for, is hardest to prove.

Verdict: keep the network as the alumni and community layer beneath The Guild, not as the whole program. On its own it does not meet the brief.

  • Say what elite means, in plain words, on the application: we invest the most in you so you rise. A welcome, not a wall. (the scholars)
  • Outcomes are named and real, and a coalition forms: placements, projects, scholars-turned-mentors; EA and ESA brought in. (Alan, Stanley)
  • Named funds stay intimate and high touch: meet-your-scholar, a story a year later, values over logos. (Sussanah)

Rules and guidelines

How The Guild actually runs

01 Eligibility

  • Students and recent alumni pursuing game craft: design, development, art, audio, production, writing, esports, or content creation.
  • A demonstrated tie to an underestimated community we serve (self-identified, in the applicant's own words), and a record of community service.
  • Undergraduate through graduate, plus a self-taught path. Craft and potential over pedigree.

02 Selection principles

  • Judge excellence, values, and trajectory, who they could become, not only what they have shipped.
  • Elite by depth, never by scarcity. We do not publicize how many we decline. No "only the few" language, ever.
  • A human decides every scholar. AI may help organize an application; it never rejects one.

03 The scholar arc

  • Scholar (year one): curriculum, triangle buddy group, a 1:1 industry mentor, GDC capstone.
  • Fellow (year two, selective): a deeper build and a paid opportunity or placement.
  • Mentor (alumni): give back to the next cohort. The give-back is a lifelong membership, not a transaction.

04 AI empowerment (the throughline)

  • Every scholar ships one AI-empowered capstone and learns human-in-the-loop direction.
  • The "AI in the Studio" module (with Take-Two) is core curriculum, taught as amplification, not replacement.
  • GGP publishes what it learns, so the program leads the field, not just its own cohort.

05 Named funds (for personal foundations)

  • A donor may name a scholarship (the Carter Lipscomb Scholars is the first, honoring a beloved mentor).
  • High touch by design: meet-your-scholar, an annual letter in the scholar's own words, a multi-year relationship.
  • Values guardrail: a named fund honors GGP's values and belonging. No condition may narrow who is welcome.

06 Corporate sponsorship

  • Tiers: named cohort, track sponsor, AI-module underwriter, mentoring seats for the sponsor's employees.
  • Recognition at the apex of the Directory; a coalition welcome (EA, ESA, and others) rather than exclusivity.
  • Outcomes dashboard: placements, projects shipped, scholars advanced to Fellow and Mentor.

07 Funder stewardship

  • Personal: intimacy, story, the human. Corporate: outcomes, brand, coalition. Two lanes, one program.
  • One 20th-anniversary narrative and one deck, so partners fund a program, not a line item.

08 Dignity and data

  • Identity self-identification is never used to exclude, only to serve and to understand who we reach.
  • Language is fixed: underestimated, not underrepresented; belonging, not DEI; gratitude and coalition, never scarcity.
  • Chosen names and pronouns are honored everywhere, from the application to the stage at GDC.

The application

Kaleidoscope questions for prospective scholars

Written to the values: dignity, belonging, AI empowerment, and the promise the room insisted on, that elite here is a welcome. Short where it can be, so the door feels open.

A · Who you are
1Your legal name, and the name you go by. Your pronouns, if you wish to share them.Chosen name and pronouns honored from the first field onward.
2Where you study or work, your level (undergraduate, graduate, recent alum, self-taught), and your location.
3In your own words, which community or communities are you part of? Self-identified, optional, and never used to exclude, only to serve.
B · Your craft
4Your discipline: design, development, art, audio, production, writing, esports, content creation, or another path.
5Share a link to your work, and describe one project you are proudest of and your exact role in it. 150 words. We read for craft and for potential, not polish alone.
6A game, tool, or experience you wish existed, and why it matters to you. 200 words.
C · AI empowerment
7How do you use AI in your creative or technical work today, and where do you want to get better? If you have not started, what would you try first? 200 words. There is no wrong answer. This is what we teach.
8AI can amplify a creator or replace one. How do you want to work with it so it makes you more yourself, not less? 150 words.
D · Community and character
9Community service is core to GGP. Describe how you show up for a community you care about. 150 words.
10Tell us about a setback and what it taught you. 200 words. We read for resilience, not perfection.
E · Fit and support
11Which path fits you best, the year-long deep track, the GDC-week track, the civic and impact track, or "help me choose"? Tell us why.
12What would this scholarship change for you? 150 words. Opportunity and need, told with dignity, never a means test.
13One reference who can speak to your character or your craft (name, relationship, contact). A second is welcome, not required.
14Anything else we should know, including any access needs, so we can support you fully.The last word is yours, and it signals we plan to meet you where you are.
